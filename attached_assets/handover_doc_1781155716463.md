# Survey Analysis Engine — Complete Reference & Handover

**Audience:** new users, stakeholders, and the in-app assistant (which is grounded on this document).
**Core promise:** every number this tool shows is computed deterministically in Python. AI is used only to phrase results and group questions — never to calculate a count, percentage, mean, or to decide whether a hypothesis is true.

---

## 1. What the tool does, in one paragraph

You upload a survey (raw responses + a data map, or a combined Excel, or raw data + a Word questionnaire). The tool reads it, works out what each question is (single-select, multi-select, grid, numeric, NPS, rank, etc.), and computes a "single cut" for every question — the breakdown of how people answered. You can then slice any question by another ("cross cuts"), split respondents into Winners vs Laggards on an outcome and see what separates them ("Outcome Segmentation"), and download a consultant-ready Excel workbook where every figure is traceable to an audit log.

---

## 2. The four steps (the journey)

1. **Upload your survey.** Drop your files. The tool auto-detects the format. If it can't, a short format wizard asks a few questions so it can read your file correctly.
2. **Decide the structure of your Excel output.** Choose whether questions are grouped into themed category sheets or kept on one sheet, set up Local Filters, and pick how cross-cuts are laid out.
3. **Run the analysis and configure.** One click computes every single cut. You then explore questions, build cross cuts, and run Outcome Segmentation.
4. **Download.** Get the workbook. The download is always available once an analysis has run.

---

## 3. Inputs the tool accepts

- **Two files:** raw data (`.csv` or `.xlsx`) + a data map (`.xlsx`).
- **One combined Excel:** raw data and data map on separate sheets.
- **Raw data + a Word questionnaire (`.docx`):** the tool builds the data map from the Word document.

A data map tells the tool what each column means: the question ID, the question text, and what each answer code stands for (e.g. `1 = Strongly agree`).

---

## 4. Question types the tool understands

- **Single-select:** pick one option (e.g. region).
- **Multi-select:** pick any number of options; each option is yes/no.
- **Grid (rated / single-select / binary):** a matrix — several row items each rated on the same scale.
- **Direct numeric:** a typed number (e.g. revenue growth %).
- **Numeric allocation:** split 100 points/% across options.
- **Rank order:** put options in preference order.
- **NPS:** 0–10 recommendation question; scored as % promoters (9–10) minus % detractors (0–6).
- **Demographic / segment:** background attributes used for slicing (region, size, role).
- **Open text / metadata:** free text or IDs; not charted.

---

## 5. Single cuts

A single cut is the answer distribution for one question: each option, how many people chose it, and the percentage. For numeric questions it's the mean/median/spread; for NPS it's the NPS score per entity.

**Reading the screen:** the selected question shows at the top with its type and valid N (how many people answered). A chart and a table follow. The display toggle switches between Count only / Count / %.

**Outlier highlighting:** cells that stand out are flagged — red for an unusually high value, amber for an unusually low one. The rule is a guarded statistical test, so flat or tiny distributions are intentionally left unflagged to avoid false alarms.

**Single pane view vs split view:** "Single pane view" stacks the single cut and its cross-cut builder in one column. Turn it off for a side-by-side workspace (single cut on the left, cross-cut builder on the right).

---

## 6. Cross cuts

A cross cut relates two questions. Examples:
- **Cross-tab:** two categorical questions → a grid of counts and row %.
- **Group comparison:** one categorical "segment" + one numeric "metric" → the mean of the metric per segment.
- **Segment profile:** the answer profile of a filtered subgroup.
- **Expected vs realized:** two numeric questions compared with their gap.

You can build one from the selected question (the inline builder beside a single cut), build one manually (choose analysis type + two questions), or run a suggested one.

---

## 7. Local Filters

Local Filters are slicers that sit at the top of each question's sheet in the Excel output. A reader can use them to restrict that sheet to a subgroup — for example, show the breakdown only for respondents where Region = APAC — without re-running anything. You choose which questions become Local Filters in step 2. They differ from the **global filter**, which restricts the entire analysis (every screen and sheet) to a subgroup.

---

## 8. Custom workbook filters & per-question filter rows

- **Custom workbook filters:** extra blank slicer slots in the workbook a partner can wire to any question across the whole workbook.
- **Per-question filter rows:** how many filter rows appear inside each question card on the dashboard, so you can filter a single question on the fly (e.g. just this question, only large firms).

---

## 9. Outcome Segmentation (Winners vs Laggards)

This is the "what separates the best from the rest" analysis. You pick an **outcome variable** (the thing that defines success — e.g. revenue growth, satisfaction, an NPS), then define who counts as a **Winner** vs a **Laggard**:

- **Categorical:** Winners are respondents who chose specific option(s).
- **Numeric threshold:** Winners scored at or above (or below) a value.
- **Quartile:** Winners are the top 25%, Laggards the bottom 25%.
- **Manual UUID upload:** you upload a list of which respondents are Winners and which are Laggards.

The tool then finds **differentiators** — the questions where Winners answer most differently from Laggards — ranked by **lift** (how many times more likely Winners are to choose an option) and **gap** (the raw percentage-point difference). It shows each with a Winner rate, Laggard rate, gap, lift, and a statistical association (Cramér's V, p-value). Every differentiator has a "Show source table" with the underlying counts.

**Why it's trustworthy:** cohorts are assigned in Python; rates and lifts are computed in Python; the AI only phrases the finding, and its phrasing is re-checked against the source numbers before you see it. A respondent who is high on one metric but low on another is not automatically a Winner — the composite logic guards against that.

**Advanced mode:** a multi-metric builder where you weight several metrics, balance cohorts across dimensions (e.g. equal region mix), and see composition charts.

## 10. Downloads

- **Single-cut workbook:** every single cut, with cross cuts inline.
- **Cross-cut-only workbook:** just the cross cuts you ticked.
- **Filtered workbook:** the per-question filtered analyses you ticked.
- **Outcome segmented workbook:** every question shown side-by-side for Winners vs Laggards.

Open outputs in **Microsoft Excel desktop** — some live formulas don't resolve in LibreOffice, Google Sheets, or Excel web.

---

## 11. AI insights — what AI does and does not do

**Does:** group questions into themes, suggest short labels, phrase a one-line takeaway for a table, phrase a hypothesis result.
**Does not:** compute any number, decide a cohort, or assert a relationship the table doesn't show. Every AI sentence that contains a number is validated against the computed table; if it doesn't match, the tool falls back to a plain description.

---

## 12. The in-app assistant (chatbot)

A sticky chat icon (bottom-right) answers three kinds of question:
1. **How the tool works** — answered from this document.
2. **About your survey** — e.g. "is there an NPS question?" — answered from the detected question list, not guesswork.
3. **Hypothesis testing** — you state a hypothesis; the assistant maps it to two questions, runs the real cross-cut engine, shows you the table, and describes only what the table shows. On small bases it hedges and labels the finding tentative. It never claims causation.

---

## 13. Trust & accuracy principles (the non-negotiables)

- Calculation-first, AI-second: Python owns every number.
- Real-data verification over green synthetic tests.
- AI output containing numbers is re-validated against source tables.
- Small bases get hedged language, never confident claims.
- Outputs are audit-traceable via the workbook's calculation log.
